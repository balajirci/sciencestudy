Quarks — the deeper layer
Quarks are fundamental particles that make up protons and neutrons. Up quarks carry charge +⅔; down quarks carry −⅓. Proton = uud (+⅔+⅔−⅓ = +1). Neutron = udd (+⅔−⅓−⅓ = 0). Quarks are bound by gluons via the strong nuclear force and are never observed in isolation (colour confinement).
⚖Atomic Mass & Isotopes
Atomic Number (Z)
Number of protons. Defines the element. Cannot change without nuclear transmutation.
Mass Number (A)
Protons + Neutrons. A = Z + N
Atomic Mass (amu)
Weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes. Not always a whole number.
Isotopes
Same element (same Z), different neutron count. Same chemical behaviour, different mass.
Hydrogen Isotopes — Protium, Deuterium, Tritium
Protium (¹H): 1p, 0n — 99.98% of natural hydrogen.
Deuterium (²H or D): 1p, 1n — used in heavy water (D&sub2;O), NMR solvents, nuclear fusion fuel (D–T reaction).
Tritium (³H): 1p, 2n — radioactive (β-decay); used in nuclear weapons and fusion research.
Atomic Mass = Σ (isotope mass × natural abundance fraction)

📈Periodic Trends
Atomic Radius
Increases down groups (more shells). Decreases left → right across a period (more protons pull electrons inward).
Ionization Energy (IE)
Energy to remove outermost e−. Increases → across period; decreases down group. Exceptions: B < Be; O < N (half-filled 2p stability).
Electronegativity
Pauling scale. F = 3.98 (highest). Increases → and upward. Decreases down and leftward. Noble gases excluded.
Electron Affinity
Energy released gaining e−. Halogens highest. Exceptions: N, Be, Mg have near-zero or positive EA (filled/half-filled subshells resist adding e−).
Practice Questions — Atomic Structure
1. What is the charge and quark composition of a neutron?
Charge = 0. Quark composition: 1 up quark (+⅔) + 2 down quarks (−⅓ each) = +⅔ − ⅓ − ⅓ = 0.
2. Deuterium has how many protons and neutrons? Name two of its uses.
1 proton, 1 neutron. Uses: (1) heavy water (D&sub2;O) — neutron moderator in CANDU nuclear reactors; (2) NMR solvents (deuterated chloroform CDCl&sub3;); (3) fuel in nuclear fusion (D–T reaction).
3. Aluminium is described as amphoteric. What does this mean? Give one reaction with a base.
Amphoteric: reacts with both acids and bases.
With NaOH: 2Al + 2NaOH + 2H&sub2;O → 2NaAlO&sub2; + 3H&sub2;↑
With HCl: 2Al + 6HCl → 2AlCl&sub3; + 3H&sub2;↑
4. Why does iodine turn starch blue-black?
I&sub2; molecules thread themselves into the helical coils of amylose (a starch component), forming a charge-transfer complex that absorbs visible light at ~600 nm, producing the characteristic deep blue-black colour. This is a classic qualitative test for starch.
5. Which element has the highest electronegativity, and what is its value on the Pauling scale?
Fluorine (F), with a Pauling electronegativity of 3.98 (often rounded to 4.0). It is the most electronegative element in the periodic table.
6. Why does ionization energy have an exception between nitrogen and oxygen (IE of O < N despite O having higher Z)?
Nitrogen has a half-filled 2p subshell (2p³ — one e− per orbital), which is extra stable due to exchange energy. Oxygen has 4 electrons in the 2p subshell, meaning one orbital has a paired electron. The repulsion between paired electrons in the same orbital makes it easier to remove one, so oxygen has a lower IE than nitrogen.

Why water has anomalously high boiling point
H&sub2;O is only 18 g/mol, yet boils at 100°C because each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds (2 as donor through its H atoms, 2 as acceptor through its lone pairs). These must all be broken to vaporise the liquid, requiring far more energy than predicted by molecular weight alone.
🔷VSEPR Theory & Molecular Geometry
VSEPR Principle
Electron pairs around a central atom repel each other and arrange to minimise repulsion. Repulsion order: lone pair–lone pair > lone pair–bonding pair > bonding pair–bonding pair. Lone pairs therefore compress bond angles below ideal values.

Hypervalence — exceeding the octet
Atoms in Period 3 and below can hold more than 8 valence electrons by using d orbitals. PCl&sub5; (10e−), SF&sub6; (12e−), XeF&sub4; (12e−). Period 2 elements (C, N, O, F) cannot be hypervalent — they have no accessible d orbitals.

Sigma (σ) Bond
End-to-end orbital overlap along the internuclear axis. Every single bond is a σ bond. First bond in any double or triple bond. Rotation around a σ bond is possible.
Pi (π) Bond
Side-to-side overlap of p orbitals, above and below the bond axis. Second bond in a double bond; 2nd & 3rd in a triple bond. Prevents rotation → causes cis/trans isomerism. Generally weaker than σ.
Bond Order Summary
Single = 1σ. Double = 1σ + 1π. Triple = 1σ + 2π. Higher bond order → shorter bond length → stronger bond.
Hückel's Rule (Aromaticity)
A cyclic, planar, fully conjugated ring with 4n+2 π electrons (n = 0, 1, 2…) is aromatic. Benzene: n=1, 6 π e−. Extra thermodynamic stability; prefers EAS not addition.
Practice Questions — Bonding & Geometry
1. What is the molecular geometry and hybridization of XeF&sub4;?
Square planar molecular geometry. XeF&sub4; has 6 electron groups (4 bonding pairs + 2 lone pairs) → octahedral electron geometry → lone pairs occupy opposite axial positions → square planar arrangement of F atoms. Hybridization: sp³d². Bond angle: 90°. Classic hypervalent molecule.
2. How many sigma and pi bonds are in a molecule of acetylene (C&sub2;H&sub2;)?
Structure: H—C≡C—H. Counting: H—C (1σ) + C≡C (1σ + 2π) + C—H (1σ) = 3 sigma bonds + 2 pi bonds. Each carbon is sp hybridized.
3. Why does water have a bent shape rather than linear? What is the exact bond angle?
Water has 4 electron groups (2 bonding pairs + 2 lone pairs) around oxygen. This gives a tetrahedral electron geometry. The 2 lone pairs exert stronger repulsion than bonding pairs, compressing the H—O—H angle from the ideal 109.5° to 104.5°. Molecular geometry = bent.
4. Metallic bonding explains electrical conductivity. Why can ionic compounds not conduct electricity as solids?
In metals, electrons are delocalized and free to move under an electric field → conductivity. In solid ionic compounds, ions are fixed in a rigid crystal lattice — they cannot move. Ionic compounds do conduct when molten or dissolved in water (ions become mobile), but not in the solid state.
5. Is pyridine (a 6-membered ring where one CH is replaced by N) aromatic? Explain using Hückel's rule.
Yes. Pyridine is cyclic, planar, and fully conjugated. The nitrogen contributes 1 p orbital to the π system (its lone pair is in an sp² orbital perpendicular to the ring — it does NOT enter the π system). Total π electrons = 6 (from 3 double bonds) = 4(1)+2 → aromatic by Hückel's rule. Compare with pyrrole where the N lone pair does enter the π system (also 6 π e−, aromatic).
Module 3

Physical Chemistry & Thermodynamics

Kinetics, activation energy, gas laws, thermodynamic state functions, phase changes, equilibrium, and Le Chatelier's principle.

⚡Reaction Kinetics
Activation Energy (E⊂a;) & Arrhenius Equation
Minimum energy required for reactants to overcome the transition-state barrier and form products. Governed by the Arrhenius equation. Higher temperature → more molecules with energy ≥ E⊂a; → faster rate.
Arrhenius: k = A · e−E⊂a;/RT   |   ln(k⊂2;/k⊂1;) = (E⊂a;/R) · (1/T⊂1; − 1/T⊂2;)
Catalysts & Enzymes
Catalyst: lowers E⊂a; by providing an alternative reaction pathway. Not consumed. Does not change ΔG, ΔH, or the equilibrium position — only the rate.

Enzyme: biological catalyst (protein, or ribozyme if RNA). Has an active site; binds substrate by lock-and-key or induced-fit model. Activity affected by pH, temperature, competitive inhibitors (block active site; overcome by more substrate) and noncompetitive inhibitors (bind allosteric site; cannot be overcome). K⊂m; = Michaelis constant = [S] at half-maximal rate (V⊂max;/2).
Rate Law
rate = k[A]m[B]n. Orders (m, n) are determined experimentally, NOT from stoichiometric coefficients (unless an elementary step).
Rate-Determining Step
Slowest step in a multi-step mechanism determines overall rate. The rate law is written from this step.
Half-life (t½)
1st order: t½ = ln2/k (constant, independent of [A]). 2nd order: t½ = 1/(k[A]⊂0;) (depends on initial concentration).
Collision Theory
Reaction requires collision with: (1) sufficient energy (≥ E⊂a;) AND (2) correct orientation (steric factor). Rate ∝ collision frequency × fraction with E⊂a; × steric factor.

When does ideal gas fail? — Van der Waals corrections
Term an²/V² (pressure correction): accounts for intermolecular attractive forces — real molecules attract each other, reducing effective impact on walls → actual P is lower than ideal.
Term −nb (volume correction): accounts for finite molecular volume — real molecules occupy space, so available volume is less than container volume.
Deviations are greatest at high pressure (molecular volume matters) and low temperature (attractive forces dominate).
🌡Thermodynamics
Enthalpy (ΔH)
Heat at constant pressure. Exothermic: ΔH < 0 (releases heat). Endothermic: ΔH > 0. Hess's Law: ΔH is path-independent.
Entropy (ΔS)
Measure of disorder / dispersal of energy. ΔS > 0 = more disorder. Boltzmann: S = k⊂B; ln(W). Third Law: S = 0 for perfect crystal at 0 K.
Gibbs Free Energy
ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. ΔG < 0: spontaneous. ΔG = 0: equilibrium. ΔG > 0: non-spontaneous. Related: ΔG = −RT ln K.
Specific Heat (c)
q = mcΔT. Water: c = 4.184 J/g·°C (highest of common liquids). Heat needed to raise 1 g by 1°C.
Absolute Zero & the Third Law
Absolute zero = 0 K = −273.15°C — the theoretical temperature at which all molecular motion ceases and entropy reaches its minimum (zero for a perfect crystal). The Third Law of Thermodynamics states it is impossible to reach absolute zero in a finite number of steps. It serves as the reference baseline for calculating absolute entropies.
Phase Changes & Heating Curves
During a phase change, temperature stays constant while energy breaks intermolecular forces (no kinetic energy change). ΔH⊂fus; (melting): ~6 kJ/mol for water. ΔH⊂vap; (boiling): ~44 kJ/mol for water — much larger because molecules must be completely separated. Sublimation: solid → gas directly (dry ice CO&sub2;, iodine at reduced pressure).
⚖Chemical Equilibrium & Le Chatelier's Principle
K⊂eq; = [products]coefficients / [reactants]coefficients    (pure solids & liquids omitted)
Le Chatelier's Principle
A system at equilibrium shifts to oppose any applied stress (concentration, pressure, temperature) and re-establish equilibrium.
Adding reactant
Q < K → forward reaction favoured → shifts toward products.
Increasing pressure (gas)
Shifts toward side with fewer moles of gas to reduce pressure.
Increasing temperature
Shifts toward endothermic direction. For exothermic reactions, K⊂eq; decreases as T rises.
Adding a catalyst
Speeds both forward and reverse reactions equally. Does NOT shift equilibrium. K⊂eq; unchanged.
Q vs K
Q < K: forward reaction favoured. Q > K: reverse favoured. Q = K: at equilibrium.
Practice Questions — Physical Chemistry & Thermodynamics
1. Van der Waals adds two correction terms to PV=nRT. What physical reality does each term correct for?
an²/V² (pressure correction): corrects for intermolecular attractive forces. Real molecules attract each other, so wall collisions are slightly weaker → actual pressure is lower than ideal. The constant ‘a’ reflects the strength of these attractions.

−nb (volume correction): corrects for the finite volume of gas molecules themselves. Real molecules are not point particles → less volume is available for free motion. Constant ‘b’ is the excluded volume per mole.
2. N⊂2;(g) + 3H⊂2;(g) ↔ 2NH⊂3;(g) + heat (Haber process). What happens to equilibrium if (a) pressure is increased, (b) temperature is increased?
(a) Increasing pressure: 4 mol gas (reactants) → 2 mol gas (products). System shifts right toward products to reduce moles of gas and lower pressure.

(b) Increasing temperature: Reaction is exothermic (ΔH < 0) — heat is a “product.” Adding heat shifts equilibrium left toward reactants. K⊂eq; decreases. (This is why industrial Haber plants use ~400–500°C as a compromise between rate and yield.)
3. For a reaction where ΔH < 0 and ΔS < 0, at what temperatures is the reaction spontaneous?
Using ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. With ΔH < 0 and ΔS < 0: at low T, the ΔH term dominates → ΔG < 0 → spontaneous. At high T, the −TΔS term becomes large and positive (since ΔS is negative), eventually making ΔG > 0 → non-spontaneous. Spontaneous at low temperatures only.
4. A first-order reaction has a rate constant k = 0.0347 min−¹. Calculate its half-life.
For a first-order reaction: t½ = ln(2) / k = 0.6931 / 0.0347 min−¹ = 19.97 ≈ 20 minutes. Note that for first-order reactions, the half-life is constant — independent of the initial concentration.

⬡Aromaticity & Benzene
Hückel's Rule: 4n+2 π electrons
A ring must be: (1) cyclic, (2) planar, (3) fully conjugated (alternating bonds, each atom has a p orbital), and (4) contain 4n+2 π electrons. Benzene: n=1 → 6 π e− ✓. Antiaromatic: 4n π electrons (e.g. cyclobutadiene, 4 π e−) — extremely unstable, worse than non-aromatic.
Benzene structure
6 sp² carbons in a ring. Each C has one unhybridized p orbital → delocalized π system. All C—C bonds equal length (1.40 Å, between single 1.54 and double 1.34 Å). Resonance-stabilized.
Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS)
Halogenation (Cl⊂2;/FeCl⊂3;), nitration (HNO⊂3;/H⊂2;SO⊂4; → adds NO⊂2;), sulfonation, Friedel–Crafts alkylation/acylation. EAS preserves aromaticity (substitution, not addition).
Activating groups (ring)
Electron-donating groups (—OH, —NH⊂2;, —OR, alkyl) activate ring toward EAS and direct to ortho/para positions.
Deactivating groups (ring)
Electron-withdrawing groups (—NO⊂2;, —COOH, —CHO, —CN) deactivate ring and direct to meta position.

Addition vs Condensation Polymerisation
Addition (chain-growth): monomers add one at a time to a growing chain; no small molecule released; all atoms of monomer appear in polymer. Requires a C=C double bond in monomer. Example: polyethylene.

Condensation (step-growth): bifunctional monomers react stepwise; small molecule (usually H⊂2;O, sometimes HCl or CH⊂3;OH) is released each time a bond forms. Example: nylon-6,6 (amide bond), PET (ester bond).
Practice Questions — Organic Chemistry
1. Why does a tertiary alkyl halide favour SN1 rather than SN2?
Steric hindrance blocks SN2: the nucleophile must attack the backside of the carbon bearing the leaving group, but three bulky alkyl groups surround the central carbon, making approach impossible.

SN1 is favoured because: the tertiary carbocation formed after the leaving group departs is highly stabilised by three alkyl groups donating electron density via hyperconjugation and induction. More stable intermediate → lower activation energy → faster ionisation step.
2. State the two structural requirements for the diene in a Diels-Alder reaction.
(1) Must be conjugated (alternating single and double bonds) to allow orbital overlap with the dienophile.
(2) Must adopt the s-cis conformation (both double bonds on the same side of the single bond). In the s-trans conformation the terminal carbons are too far apart (~3.9 Å) to reach both ends of the dienophile. Ideally the dienophile has electron-withdrawing groups (C=O, NO⊂2;, CN) to lower its LUMO energy.
3. Predict the product when (R)-2-bromobutane reacts with NaOH in DMSO. What is the stereochemistry?
Conditions: strong nucleophile (OH−), polar aprotic solvent (DMSO) → SN2 reaction. Backside attack at C-2 causes Walden inversion. Starting material is (R) → product is (S)-2-butanol. Complete inversion, no racemization (SN2 is stereospecific).
4. What bond forms in nylon-6,6 and what small molecule is released during its synthesis?
An amide bond (—CO—NH—) forms each time a carboxylic acid group of adipic acid reacts with an amine group of hexamethylenediamine. Water (H⊂2;O) is released as the by-product. This is condensation polymerisation. The “6,6” refers to the 6 carbons in each monomer.
5. Markovnikov's rule states that in the addition of HX to an alkene, the H adds to which carbon?
H+ adds to the carbon that already bears more hydrogens (the less substituted carbon). Equivalently, X− adds to the more substituted carbon (forming the more stable, more substituted carbocation intermediate). Example: HBr + propene → H adds to C1 (CH⊂2;=), Br adds to C2 → 2-bromopropane (major product), not 1-bromopropane.

📡Mass Spectrometry
How it works — four stages
(1) Ionisation — electron impact: molecule loses e− to give M+• radical cation. Modern methods: ESI (soft, no fragmentation), MALDI (for large biomolecules).
(2) Acceleration — ions accelerated through electric field; all given same kinetic energy.
(3) Deflection — magnetic or electric field deflects ions; lighter ions deflect more (smaller m/z).
(4) Detection — ions detected; spectrum shows relative abundance vs m/z.
Molecular Ion Peak (M+)
Highest m/z peak (excluding isotope peaks) = molecular weight of compound. When z=1, m/z = mass in g/mol.
Base Peak
Tallest peak (100% relative abundance) = most stable fragment. Not necessarily M+.
Isotope Peaks (M+1, M+2)
Cl: M+2 ≈ M (3:1 ratio, ³&sup5;Cl:³&sup7;Cl). Br: M ≈ M+2 (1:1, &sup7;&sup9;Br:&sup8;¹Br). These patterns identify halogens.
Fragmentation patterns
Loss of 15 = CH⊂3;. Loss of 29 = CHO or C⊂2;H⊂5;. Loss of 45 = OEt. Loss of 77 = C⊂6;H⊂5; (phenyl ring).

Principles of ¹H NMR
Protons in different chemical environments absorb radiofrequency radiation at different resonance frequencies in a magnetic field. Key observables: Chemical shift (δ, ppm) — position relative to TMS (δ=0); Multiplicity — splitting by neighbouring H (n+1 rule); Integration — area ∝ number of equivalent protons.

n+1 Splitting Rule
A proton with n equivalent neighbouring protons splits into n+1 peaks. 0 neighbours = singlet (s); 1 = doublet (d); 2 = triplet (t); 3 = quartet (q).

Beer–Lambert Law (UV-Vis)
A = εlc. Absorbance (A) = molar absorptivity (ε) × path length (l, cm) × concentration (c, mol/L). Used for quantitative analysis of chromophores.
Bragg's Law (X-ray)
nλ = 2d sinθ. Used in X-ray crystallography to determine crystal plane spacing (d) and ultimately 3D molecular structure.
Practice Questions — Lab Techniques & Analytical
1. In agarose gel electrophoresis of DNA, do larger or smaller fragments migrate farther? Explain why.
Smaller fragments migrate farther (farther from the well toward the positive electrode). All DNA fragments carry negative charge proportional to their size (phosphate backbone), so all move toward + electrode. However, smaller fragments encounter less resistance moving through the agarose gel matrix and therefore travel a greater distance in the same time. A DNA ladder (size standards) loaded in a parallel lane allows size determination by comparison.
2. A ¹H NMR shows a singlet at δ 9.8 ppm and the molecular formula contains one oxygen. What functional group does this suggest, and why is the peak so far downfield?
This indicates an aldehyde (—CHO). The aldehydic proton appears at ~9–10 ppm because the adjacent carbonyl group strongly deshields it through both induction (electronegative O withdraws electron density) and anisotropic effects of the C=O π system. It typically appears as a singlet (no adjacent CH) or a doublet if adjacent to a CH. A carboxylic acid (COOH) would appear at ~10–12 ppm as a broad singlet.
3. The M+ peak appears at m/z = 136 and there is an equally intense M+2 peak. What element does this pattern indicate?
An approximately equal M and M+2 ratio (1:1) is the classic signature of bromine. Natural bromine is ~50% &sup7;&sup9;Br and ~50% &sup8;¹Br (differ by 2 mass units), giving nearly equal M+ and M+2 peaks. By contrast, chlorine (3:1 M:M+2 ratio) gives an M+2 peak at about one-third the height of M+. The molecular weight would be ~136 (the lighter isotopologue).
4. You want to separate a mixture of compounds with boiling points 78°C, 80°C, and 115°C. Would you use simple or fractional distillation? Why?
Fractional distillation is required. Simple distillation works well only when boiling points differ by more than ~25°C. The first two compounds (78°C and 80°C) differ by only 2°C and cannot be separated by simple distillation — they would co-distil. A fractionating column creates multiple vaporisation–condensation cycles (theoretical plates), allowing effective separation of closely boiling liquids. The third compound (115°C) could be separated from the first two by either method.

Conjugate Acid–Base Pairs (Brønsted–Lowry)
When an acid donates H+, the remaining species is its conjugate base (one fewer H+). When a base accepts H+, it becomes its conjugate acid.
Example: CH⊂3;COOH + H⊂2;O ↔ CH⊂3;COO− + H⊂3;O+
• CH⊂3;COOH / CH⊂3;COO− are a conjugate pair. • H⊂2;O / H⊂3;O+ are a conjugate pair.
Key rule: A strong acid has a weak conjugate base. A weak acid has a relatively strong conjugate base.
📊pH, K⊂a;, and pK⊂a;
pH = −log[H⊂3;O+]   |   pOH = −log[OH−]   |   pH + pOH = 14 (at 25°C)
K⊂a; = [H+][A−]/[HA]   |   pK⊂a; = −log(K⊂a;)   |   K⊂w; = [H+][OH−] = 1.0×10−¹&sup4; at 25°C
Strong Acids (fully dissociate)
HCl, HBr, HI, HNO⊂3;, H⊂2;SO⊂4; (first H only), HClO⊂4;. These 6 must be memorised. [H+] = initial acid concentration.
Strong Bases
Group 1 hydroxides (LiOH, NaOH, KOH) and heavier Group 2 (Ca(OH)⊂2;, Sr(OH)⊂2;, Ba(OH)⊂2;). Fully dissociate.
Weak Acid Examples & pK⊂a;
Acetic acid: pK⊂a; 4.74. HF: pK⊂a; 3.17. Carbonic acid H⊂2;CO⊂3;: pK⊂a;1 6.35. NH⊂4;+: pK⊂a; 9.25. Phenol: pK⊂a; 9.99.
Acid Strength Factors
Stronger acid = more stable conjugate base. Increased by: larger atom size (HI > HBr > HCl), more electronegative substituents (inductive effect), resonance stabilisation of A− (carboxylates), oxidation state (HClO⊂4; > HClO⊂3;).
🔄Buffers & Henderson–Hasselbalch Equation
What is a buffer?
A buffer resists pH change when small amounts of strong acid or base are added. It consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base + conjugate acid) present in comparable concentrations. When H+ is added: conjugate base (A−) absorbs it → HA. When OH− is added: weak acid (HA) neutralises it → A− + H⊂2;O. Effective buffering range: pK⊂a; ± 1.
Henderson–Hasselbalch: pH = pK⊂a; + log([A−]/[HA])
When [A−] = [HA]
log(1) = 0 → pH = pK⊂a;. This is the half-equivalence point in a titration. Maximum buffer capacity.
Buffer Capacity
Greatest when [A−] = [HA] (ratio 1:1). Also increases with higher total concentration of both species. Fails when strong acid/base added exceeds the buffer components.
Blood Buffer System
CO⊂2;/HCO⊂3;− (pK⊂a; 6.1, effective pH 7.35–7.45 adjusted by breathing rate). Also: haemoglobin buffer, phosphate buffer. Acidosis: pH < 7.35. Alkalosis: pH > 7.45.
Acetate Buffer (lab example)
CH⊂3;COOH / CH⊂3;COO− (pK⊂a; 4.74). Effective pH range: 3.74–5.74. Prepared by mixing acetic acid and sodium acetate.
🧪Solubility & Precipitation
Solubility Product K⊂sp;
K⊂sp; is the equilibrium constant for the dissolution of a sparingly soluble ionic salt. For AgCl ↔ Ag+ + Cl−: K⊂sp; = [Ag+][Cl−] = 1.8×10−¹&sup0;. If ion product Q > K⊂sp; → precipitate forms. If Q < K⊂sp; → solution is unsaturated, no precipitate.

Common Ion Effect
Adding an ion that is already present in the equilibrium shifts solubility equilibrium to the left → reduces solubility. Example: Adding NaCl to saturated AgCl in water. Extra Cl− → Q > K⊂sp; → more AgCl precipitates. This is Le Chatelier's principle applied to solubility. Used in gravimetric analysis to ensure complete precipitation.
Practice Questions — Acids, Bases & Solutions
1. Calculate the pH of a buffer made from 0.10 M acetic acid and 0.20 M sodium acetate (pK⊂a; of acetic acid = 4.74).
Using Henderson–Hasselbalch:
pH = pK⊂a; + log([A−]/[HA]) = 4.74 + log(0.20/0.10) = 4.74 + log(2) = 4.74 + 0.301 = 5.04
The pH is above the pK⊂a; because there is twice as much conjugate base as weak acid — the solution is slightly more basic than the half-equivalence point.
2. BF⊂3; is a Lewis acid but not a Brønsted–Lowry acid. Explain this distinction.
Lewis acid: BF⊂3; has an empty p orbital on boron and can accept an electron pair from a Lewis base (e.g., NH⊂3; donates its lone pair → F⊂3;B:NH⊂3; adduct). It is an electron pair acceptor.

Not a Brønsted–Lowry acid: BF⊂3; contains no hydrogen atoms at all, so it cannot donate a proton (H+). Brønsted–Lowry theory is strictly about proton transfer. Lewis theory is broader — it encompasses all electron-pair interactions, including those with no proton involved.
3. Will a precipitate form when 50 mL of 0.001 M AgNO⊂3; is mixed with 50 mL of 0.001 M NaCl? (K⊂sp; of AgCl = 1.8×10−¹&sup0;)
Step 1 — find concentrations after mixing: Total volume = 100 mL. Concentrations halve: [Ag+] = [Cl−] = 5×10−&sup4; M.

Step 2 — calculate Q: Q = [Ag+][Cl−] = (5×10−&sup4;)(5×10−&sup4;) = 2.5×10−&sup7;

Step 3 — compare Q to K⊂sp;: Q (2.5×10−&sup7;) >> K⊂sp; (1.8×10−¹&sup0;) → Yes, a white precipitate of AgCl forms.
4. List the six strong acids. Why are these six in particular considered “strong”?
The six strong acids: HCl (hydrochloric), HBr (hydrobromic), HI (hydroiodic), HNO⊂3; (nitric), H⊂2;SO⊂4; (sulfuric, first proton only), HClO⊂4; (perchloric).

Why strong? They dissociate essentially 100% in water because their conjugate bases are extremely stable (very weak bases): Cl−, Br−, I− are large, polarisable anions that delocalise charge effectively; NO⊂3;−, HSO⊂4;−, ClO⊂4;− are stabilised by resonance and the high oxidation state of the central atom. A stronger conjugate base = weaker acid; a weaker conjugate base = stronger acid.
5. A weak acid HA has pK⊂a; = 5.0. What ratio of [A−]/[HA] is needed to make a buffer at pH 5.5?
pH = pK⊂a; + log([A−]/[HA])
5.5 = 5.0 + log([A−]/[HA])
log([A−]/[HA]) = 0.5
[A−]/[HA] = 10&sup0;·&sup5; = 3.16
So the conjugate base concentration must be approximately 3.16 times greater than the weak acid concentration. This is within the effective buffering range (pK⊂a; ± 1 = pH 4–6).

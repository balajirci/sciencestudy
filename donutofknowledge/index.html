The Donut of Knowledge: a field guide

Dominic Walliman's map arranges all human knowledge in concentric rings: the most abstract disciplines at the core, the most applied at the edge. Each ring depends on the one inside it.

Formal sciences
The innermost ring. Pure logic, mathematics, computer science. Truths established by proof, not experiment.
Natural sciences
Physics, chemistry, biology -- the empirical core. They explain how the universe actually behaves.
Applied sciences
Medicine, engineering, meteorology. Natural science knowledge put to practical human use.
Social sciences
Economics, psychology, sociology -- studying human behaviour and collective systems.
Arts and humanities
The outermost ring. Meaning, culture, expression -- irreducible to equations alone.
The backbone
Philosophy and the scientific method -- the roots from which all other disciplines grow.

The core idea: Knowledge is not a flat list of subjects. It forms a nested structure -- each outer ring borrows the tools and language of the rings inside it. A doctor (applied) uses biology (natural) which uses chemistry which uses physics which uses mathematics (formal). The donut makes this dependency visible.

Why a donut, not a pyramid? A pyramid implies hierarchy -- that maths matters more than poetry. The ring shape says something subtler: every discipline is fully legitimate at its boundary, but each one rests on what is inside it. Nothing is above or below -- only closer to abstraction or closer to human life.

Formal sciences
Innermost ring -- pure abstract structure
Mathematics
The language of quantity, pattern, and structure. Gives every other science its grammar.
Logic
The rules of valid reasoning. Underpins both mathematics and philosophy.
Statistics
Reasoning under uncertainty. The bridge between raw data and reliable conclusions.
Computer science
The science of computation and information -- rooted in mathematical logic.

Feeds into

Physics Chemistry Engineering Economics Medicine (data)

Formal sciences are unique: their truths are established by proof, not experiment. A theorem proven in 300 BC is still valid today. This makes them the most stable foundation -- every other discipline borrows their rigor while adding the empirical messiness of its own domain.

Natural sciences
Empirical core -- how the universe actually works
Physics
The most fundamental -- forces, energy, space-time. All natural sciences ultimately reduce to it.
Chemistry
How atoms bond and react. Built on quantum physics; builds into biology.
Biology
Life as an emergent chemical phenomenon. Chemistry with memory and purpose.
Earth science
Geology, oceanography -- physics and chemistry of a planet over deep time.
Astronomy
Physics at the largest scales -- stars, galaxies, cosmology.
Ecology
Biology of interacting populations and environments.

Draws from

Mathematics Statistics Scientific method

Feeds into

Medicine Engineering Environmental science Meteorology

The reductionist ladder: Biology reduces to chemistry, chemistry reduces to physics, physics (we hope) reduces to mathematics. This does not mean biology is "just chemistry" -- emergent complexity at each level creates genuinely new phenomena that cannot be predicted from the level below alone.

Applied sciences
Natural science harnessed for human ends
Medicine
Applied biology and chemistry -- understanding and repairing the human body.
Engineering
Applied physics and mathematics -- designing systems that work in the real world.
Neuroscience
The biology of the brain -- bridging natural science and psychology.
Meteorology
Applied fluid dynamics and thermodynamics -- predicting the atmosphere.
Materials science
Chemistry and physics of solids -- enabling everything from chips to bridges.
Public health
Medicine at population scale -- where biology meets sociology.

Key crossovers

Nutrition and social policy Neuroscience and psychology Architecture and design Engineering and computer science

Applied sciences are where knowledge becomes capability. A physicist discovers how transistors work; an engineer designs a chip; a computer scientist writes software; a doctor uses that software for diagnosis. Each step translates inner-ring knowledge into outer-ring use.

Social sciences
Human behaviour and collective systems
Psychology
The science of mind and behaviour -- bridge between biology and society.
Economics
Decision-making under scarcity -- uses heavy mathematics from the formal ring.
Sociology
How groups, institutions, and norms emerge from individual behaviour.
Political science
Power, governance, and collective decision-making at scale.
Geography
How physical space shapes human activity -- spans natural and social sciences.
Linguistics
Structure and use of language -- connects psychology, biology, and culture.
Anthropology
Human cultures across time and space -- bridges biology and humanities.
Education
How knowledge is transmitted -- applied psychology at civilisational scale.

The hardest sciences? Social sciences study systems where the subjects can read and respond to theories about themselves. An electron does not change its behaviour upon learning quantum mechanics. Humans do -- making social science both harder and stranger than physics.

Arts and humanities
Outermost ring -- meaning, expression, and culture
Art
Visual creation -- the oldest form of human expression and meaning-making.
Music
Structured sound over time -- deeply mathematical yet irreducibly experiential.
Literature
Language as art -- narrative, metaphor, imagination beyond the literal.
Theatre and cinema
Collaborative storytelling -- synthesis of nearly every other art form.
History
The record and interpretation of human events over time.
Architecture
Where engineering meets aesthetics -- applied physics in service of beauty.
Law
Formalised rules for human coexistence -- applied philosophy and political science.
Gender studies
Critical examination of identity and power as social constructs.

Why arts belong on the donut: The humanities are not decoration on top of "real" knowledge. They are the domain in which humans have always decided what knowledge is for. Ethics, meaning, and beauty cannot be derived from equations -- they are the questions that motivate the equations in the first place.

The backbone
Philosophy and the scientific method -- roots of all knowledge
ph
Philosophy
The original discipline. Before physics, chemistry, or mathematics were separate, there was philosophy -- asking how we can know anything at all. Aristotle appears on the map for good reason.
L
Logic
Valid inference -- the rules that prevent contradictions. Belongs simultaneously to philosophy and formal science.
R
Rationalism
The view that reason alone can yield knowledge. Gives mathematics its authority independent of experiment.
E
Empiricism
The view that knowledge comes from observation and experience. Gives the natural sciences their method.
H
Testable hypotheses
The key criterion of science: a claim must be falsifiable. This is what separates science from other forms of knowing.
O
Objectivity and reductionism
Two postures science takes -- removing the observer's bias, and explaining complex things via their simpler components.

The invisible infrastructure: The backbone items are not themselves sciences -- they are the epistemological commitments that make science possible. Every researcher implicitly operates inside these assumptions every day. Walliman includes them as a reminder that all of science rests on philosophical choices made centuries ago.
